📖 Introduction

Buckle up — this topic is everywhere right now. Your manager is talking about it. The news won't stop covering it. Half your LinkedIn feed is AI posts (some of them written by AI). And yes, you're probably being measured on how much you use it.

But here's the thing: most of the hype is built on misunderstanding. People either think AI is magic — a digital brain that thinks and reasons — or they're terrified it's going to take over the world. The truth is more interesting and more useful than either extreme.

🧠 What is AI? (And What It Isn't)

Let's start with the honest version. When Todd opened these sessions, he said something that sticks: "I don't believe AI exists. I use the terminology because that's what people expect. But big air quotes around intelligence."

Artificial Intelligence is an umbrella term for a field that has evolved since the 1950s, encompassing strategies and techniques for making machines perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. That definition, directly from the course slides, is exactly right — but notice what it does not say. It does not say the machines are intelligent. It says they perform tasks that typically require intelligence. That's a crucial distinction.

💡 Todd's preferred term: Pseudo-intelligence — borrowed from Neal Stephenson's sci-fi novel The Diamond Age. It looks intelligent. It produces outputs that follow human patterns. But it does not think, reason, or possess consciousness. "Pseudo" is honest: it makes no false claim of genuine intelligence.

What AI Actually Does

Imagine your phone's autocomplete feature. Every time you type, it predicts what word comes next based on patterns it has seen before. That is the core of what every modern AI system does — just at a vastly larger, more sophisticated scale.

AI models do not think. They calculate probabilities. They predict what the next most likely output should be, based on patterns learned from training data. There is no understanding, no consciousness, no intent. It is, at its heart, a very fancy pattern-matching engine.

Think about what that means in practice:

  • It looks backwards, not forwards. AI models are trained on past data. They can remix and recombine what humans have already created, but they do not engage with the world, generate truly novel ideas from first principles, or notice when something new happens outside their training window.
  • It has no continuity. If you chat with an AI today and come back in three days, it did not sit there waiting for you. It has no sense of time passing. Each conversation starts from zero unless you explicitly provide context.
  • It is not a moral entity. It cannot be held responsible. It has no values (though it can be tuned to express them). Responsibility always lies with the humans building and deploying it.

Examples of AI in the Real World

AI shows up in more places than people realise. The course slides list three broad categories:

  • Computer Vision — teaching machines to interpret images and video. Used in self-driving cars, medical imaging, security cameras, and the plant identifier apps on your phone.
  • Smart Devices — voice assistants like Alexa, smart thermostats, recommendation engines on Netflix and Spotify.
  • Machine Learning (including Generative AI) — from spam filters and fraud detection to ChatGPT and image generators.

⚡ Historical note: Alan Turing in the 1950s already taught a Manchester computer to compose poetry. The concept of AI is not new — it has just become vastly more powerful as computing power, data availability, and specialized chips (GPUs, TPUs) have grown.

⚙️ Machine Learning Fundamentals

Machine learning is the engine under the hood of most modern AI. Once you understand it, the magic disappears — replaced by something more useful: a clear mental model of how these systems actually work.

Machine Learning is a group of AI techniques that use existing data to train a mathematical model. The model learns to recognize patterns in the training data so it can make an accurate prediction when faced with new input data.

Figure 3: The ML lifecycle — training creates the model, inference uses it.

Training vs. Inference

These are the two main phases of any ML system and they are completely different operations:

  • Training — You feed the model labeled examples (training data) over and over. Each time, it makes a prediction, checks how wrong it was, and adjusts its internal numbers (parameters or weights) to be less wrong next time. The data, techniques, and models you choose drive the quality and value of the outputs. Garbage in, garbage out — always.

  • Inference — Inference is the output the model produces by applying what it has learned during training to new input data. At this point the model is frozen. It is not learning anymore — it is just applying its learned patterns to produce a prediction.

💡 The oven analogy: Training is like preheating an oven. The oven (model) needs time to reach temperature (learn). Once it is preheated, you just put food in (inference). You are not re-heating the oven every time you cook a meal.

How Models Actually Learn: A Pizza Example

Todd demonstrated this beautifully in the SageMaker session. He trained a model to classify images as "pizza" or "not pizza" using about 2,000 images.

Here is what happens step by step:

  1. Show the untrained model a picture of pizza. It has never seen anything — it guesses randomly.
  2. Tell it: "Wrong — that's pizza." The model notes: round shape, red and orange tones, certain textures.
  3. Show it a soccer ball (round, black and white patches). It guesses pizza. "Wrong — that's not pizza." Now it learns: not just round things.
  4. Repeat thousands of times. Each round, the model adjusts its internal numbers until its pizza predictions are reliable.

⚡ What the model "sees": Not pepperoni, cheese, or crust. It sees statistical patterns in 32-bit floating-point numbers representing pixel values. It does not know what food is. It has learned which combinations of numbers tend to accompany the label "pizza."

This is also why models can fail in surprising ways. Todd found that a PNG image with an alpha transparency bit set — visually identical to pizza — completely broke the model. Models are only as capable as the variants covered in their training data. An input format the model has never seen results in undefined behavior.

Overfitting: The Memorisation Trap

Overfitting occurs when a model learns the training data too precisely — including its noise — and fails to generalise correctly to new, unseen examples.

Todd gave a perfect analogy for this: imagine studying for a certification by memorising practice question answers rather than understanding the concepts. You know the answer to question 47 is "C." But the real exam has different wording, different answer order, different distractors. You fail — because you memorised answers, not concepts.

An overfit model does the same. It can predict everything in its training set perfectly, but shows up and fails when it sees something slightly different. The fix: more diverse training data, regularisation techniques, and proper validation using a held-out test set.

When Should You Use ML?

ML is not always the right tool. Before committing to it, run through this decision framework:

Figure 4: Decision tree for choosing between traditional analytics and ML.

Stick with traditional analytics if:

  • Business rules can be hardcoded
  • Variables are limited and well-defined
  • Statistical modelling solves the problem
  • Data analytics provides answers fast enough

Consider ML when:

  • You have enough relevant, high-quality training data
  • ML can provide the required level of accuracy, transparency, and speed
  • Your organisation has the skills and resources to build and sustain an ML solution
  • The cost of ML is justified by the problem's impact

✨ Generative AI & Foundation Models

If regular ML is about prediction (is this pizza or not?), generative AI is about creation — generating new content in response to a prompt. It is built on top of the same ML principles but operates at a scale that was unimaginable a decade ago.

Foundation Models (FMs)

The key concept underlying modern generative AI is the foundation model. Unlike older ML models trained for a single narrow task (play chess, detect spam), a foundation model is trained on an enormous, diverse dataset and can be applied to a wide range of tasks through prompting or fine-tuning.

Figure 5: Generative AI flow — prompt in, new content out, through a pre-trained foundation model.

Large Language Models (LLMs)

LLMs are foundation models pre-trained extensively on massive amounts of text — trillions of words — making them able to generate a wide variety of text content.

LLM capabilities include:

  • Text generation — writing essays, emails, blog posts
  • Question and answer — retrieving information conversationally
  • Summarisation — condensing long documents
  • Text extraction — pulling specific facts from unstructured text
  • Conversations — multi-turn chat
  • Code generation — writing working code from a natural language description

💡 Why "large"? Modern LLMs have billions or even trillions of parameters — the numerical values the model adjusts during training. GPT-3 has 175 billion. This scale is what allows the model to capture the nuance of human language.

What is a Prompt?

A prompt is an instruction or question written in natural language text that requests the generative AI model to perform a specific task.

Prompts can be simple or highly engineered. Different model categories respond to different kinds of prompts:

  • Language model: "Summarise the distinctions between dogs and cats."
  • Image model: "Generate an image of a black puppy with large ears and a white kitten playing with a ball."
  • Audio model: "Generate the sound of a joyful puppy barking."
  • Biomedical model: "Predict the three-dimensional structure of the canine olfactory receptor protein OR5B17."

Hallucination: The Confidence Problem

One of the most important limitations to understand is hallucination. Because LLMs generate the most statistically plausible next output rather than looking up verified facts, they can produce confident, well-written answers that are completely false.

The course slides give a real and damaging example: lawyers submitting court documents containing fake case citations generated by AI tools. The model generates what is plausible, not what is true — always verify AI-generated facts before relying on them.

❌ Common mistake: Treating AI output as ground truth. AI is a starting point for research, not a replacement for verification. Always spot-check citations, facts, and code before using them.

🛡️ Responsible AI

This was the section Todd spent the most time on across both sessions — and for good reason. Technical skills are table stakes. What distinguishes a professional AI practitioner is their ethical compass.

💡 As Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. is quoted: "We have guided missiles and misguided men." The tools are powerful. The question is whether the humans wielding them have thought carefully about their use.

The Three Pillars of Responsible AI

The course slides define three core requirements for any responsible AI system:

Figure 6: The three pillars of responsible AI practice.

  • Accurate, Fair, Safe, and Secure: Keep everyone and their data safe, and make sure that output is valid and fair. This includes ensuring training data is representative and unbiased, that the system does not expose sensitive data, and that its outputs are accurate enough to be relied upon.

  • Explainable and Transparent: Provide mechanisms to validate and audit what is happening behind the scenes. Empower stakeholders to make informed choices about their interactions with an AI system. If you cannot explain why the system made a decision, you cannot catch when it is wrong.

  • Governed and Controlled: Incorporate responsible best practices across the full scope of an AI system. Provide mechanisms to steer AI system behavior. This means policies, monitoring, human oversight, and the ability to intervene.

AI Bias: A Real, Serious Problem

Bias in AI is not a theoretical concern. It is happening now, in systems people trust, with real consequences.

Healthcare: Studies have found that some healthcare algorithms demonstrate racial bias in treatment recommendations — patients from certain groups may not receive the care they need because the model was trained on data that under-represented them.

Facial recognition: Systems trained predominantly on one demographic perform significantly worse on others. This has led to documented cases of wrongful identification — someone being accused of a crime because a biased facial recognition system could not distinguish between individuals from an under-represented group.

Transcription: AI transcription trained on a particular accent or dialect (often a neutral American English accent) performs poorly on speakers who do not fit that pattern — misrepresenting their words, sometimes in high-stakes contexts.

❌ Root cause of bias: Bias in AI systems begins with the training data. If the training data does not represent the full diversity of the population the system will serve, the model will perform inequitably.

The Hidden Cost: Human Labellers

Modern AI systems require vast amounts of labelled training data. Much of that labelling is done by humans — often in poor working conditions, for low pay, sometimes reviewing deeply disturbing content during content moderation work. This human cost is largely invisible when people talk about "AI-powered" systems.

As a practitioner, this is part of the ethical landscape you are operating in. The supply chain of AI includes human labour, and advocating for fair treatment of those workers is part of being a responsible practitioner.

Practical Disclosure: Todd's Approach

Todd shared his own personal practice for responsible AI use — simple, actionable, and worth adopting:

  • Always disclose AI use. Every piece of content he generates with AI has a disclaimer: "Drafted with AI, posted by [person]" or "Scheduled with AI — please verify your availability."
  • This prompts verification. When people know AI was involved, they apply extra scrutiny — catching errors (like wrong timezone calculations) that might otherwise slip through.
  • It builds trust. Transparency about AI use, rather than hiding it, builds more durable professional credibility.

✅ Best practice: Before using AI for any task, ask: Do I need to use AI at all? Is generative AI the right tool, or would a simpler approach work? If I do use it, have I verified the output? Have I disclosed it where appropriate?

Spotting AI-Generated Content

Todd spent a fun few minutes on the tells — the patterns that reveal AI-generated text. Useful both for spotting others' AI use and for making your own AI-assisted work sound more human:

  • Capital letters — AI is grammatically meticulous; humans in a hurry often skip the shift key
  • M-dashes — AI loves the em dash (—); humans often use a hyphen (-)
  • The word "delve" — almost never used by humans organically; AI uses it constantly
  • "It's not X, it's Y" constructions — a favourite AI sentence structure
  • Excessive bullet points and bolding — AI over-formats; humans write more naturally in prose
  • Over-explanation — AI expands everything to maximum length; humans are often more succinct

☁️ The AWS AI/ML Stack

Now that you understand what AI is conceptually, let's map it to AWS. The cloud practitioner model of IaaS → PaaS → SaaS applies perfectly to the AWS AI/ML ecosystem.

Figure 7: The AWS AI/ML stack — three layers of abstraction from SageMaker to Bedrock to Q/PartyRock.

Amazon SageMaker — The Infrastructure Layer

SageMaker is the bottom of the stack — analogous to IaaS. SageMaker gives you full control over compute, datasets, model training pipelines, and deployment endpoints. This is where serious ML engineers live when they need to build custom models from scratch.

Key SageMaker capabilities:

  • SageMaker Studio — A full IDE for ML development with Jupyter notebooks
  • SageMaker Canvas — A visual, no-code GUI for building and training models (great for getting hands-on without heavy coding)
  • Data Wrangler — Visual data preparation and ETL pipeline builder
  • Training jobs — Managed compute for training models; quick jobs take 15-30 minutes, standard jobs can take hours to 24 hours and use ensemble methods for better accuracy
  • Model endpoints — Deployed models become API endpoints you can call from your applications

💰 Cost warning: SageMaker is not covered by free tier for most features. Training jobs incur compute charges. Do not run training jobs in a sandbox or free account without understanding the cost first.

Amazon Bedrock — The Middle Layer

Bedrock is the middle layer — analogous to PaaS. Bedrock abstracts away training infrastructure while giving you a choice of foundation models from AWS and third-party providers through a single managed API.

What Bedrock gives you:

  • Access to multiple FMs — AWS Nova models, Anthropic Claude, Meta Llama, and more
  • Custom model fine-tuning without managing training infrastructure
  • Agentic capabilities (Bedrock Agents) for building multi-step AI workflows
  • Knowledge bases (RAG) for grounding model responses in your own documents
  • Model evaluation tools

PartyRock is built on top of Bedrock, and Amazon Q also uses Bedrock capabilities internally.

Amazon PartyRock — Free Playground

PartyRock is a free, browser-based playground built on Bedrock. It requires no code, no AWS account, and no infrastructure setup. You can:

  • Try different foundation models interactively
  • Build simple AI applications with a visual interface
  • Experiment with prompt engineering
  • Share apps with others

✅ Start here: If you have never used an AI model API before, PartyRock is the best starting point. It is free, safe, and lets you understand how prompts and models interact before you start writing code or incurring AWS costs. Log in with your Amazon, Google, or Apple account at partyrock.aws.

Amazon Q (Quick) — The Agentic Layer

Amazon Q (referred to as "Quick" in these sessions) sits at the top of the stack — a software-as-a-service-level AI assistant. It is an agentic tool that can:

  • Summarise and compare documents (Todd used it to compare exam results across six CSV files in under 30 seconds)
  • Schedule meetings by reading calendar data and sending invites
  • Post to Slack with context from your work
  • Take notes from meetings
  • Help prioritise tasks for people who struggle with executive function

Todd described Amazon Q as "a non-pharmaceutical intervention for untreated ADHD" — a tool that handles the cognitive overhead of information management so you can focus on the work that matters.

⚖️ The Promise and the Challenge

Todd is what he called a "skeptical optimist" — someone who believes in AI's potential while being clear-eyed about its risks. That balance is worth holding onto as you navigate this field.

The Promise

The course slides describe real, meaningful applications of AI for good:

  • AI can perform complex analysis across massive amounts of data including sounds, images, and environmental information — at a scale impossible for human teams alone.
  • AI allows organisations to identify and respond to issues that would otherwise require vastly larger teams of human experts.
  • Organisations are using AI to address global challenges including wildlife protection, food insecurity, and disaster recovery.

On a personal scale, Todd described AI tools that help him manage ADHD — handling the cognitive overhead of scheduling, note-taking, and task prioritisation so his attention can go where it matters most. That's a meaningful human benefit that does not require AI to be "intelligent" — just useful.

The Challenge

The challenge section of the slides is equally important. Negative impacts can be serious when AI is not used responsibly.

Beyond the bias and hallucination issues covered earlier, the broader societal challenges include:

  • Data privacy: Agentic AI systems with access to your calendar, emails, and documents create new attack surfaces and trust questions. You often cannot see inside the model or audit what it is doing with your data.
  • Job displacement: AI tools that automate tasks create genuine economic disruption. As a practitioner, you are partly responsible for thinking through whether the tool you are building will cost someone their livelihood — and whether that trade-off is justified.
  • Energy and environmental impact: Training and running large AI models requires enormous amounts of electricity and cooling water. Data centres are often sited in communities with limited power to resist, creating environmental justice concerns.
  • Concentration of power: The infrastructure for AI (training compute, data, distribution) is concentrated in a small number of very large companies. This creates structural power imbalances that regulation is only beginning to address.

⚡ Todd's framing: "We are the ones who are at the forefront of that. Take that responsibility really, really seriously." As AWS-trained practitioners, you have more agency over these outcomes than the average person. Use it thoughtfully.

📋 Overall Summary

These two sessions covered the philosophical and conceptual foundations of AI — the ground floor that everything else in the programme builds on.

The core ideas to carry forward:

  • AI is pattern matching, not intelligence. It predicts statistically likely outputs. It does not think, reason, or understand. "Pseudo-intelligence" is a more honest term.
  • ML is a subset of AI. Training builds a model from data. Inference applies that model to new inputs. The quality of the output is entirely determined by the quality of the training data and the choice of techniques.
  • Generative AI is built on foundation models. LLMs are trained on trillions of words and respond to natural language prompts. They can generate text, code, images, and more — but they hallucinate, so always verify.
  • Responsible AI is not optional. Bias, hallucination, privacy risks, and labour ethics are not edge cases — they are inherent properties of current AI systems that practitioners must actively manage.
  • The AWS AI/ML stack has three layers: SageMaker (full control, IaaS-like), Bedrock (managed models, PaaS-like), and Q/PartyRock (end-user tools, SaaS-like). Start with PartyRock for free exploration.

💡 Key Takeaways

  • AI has been around since the 1950s — Alan Turing was teaching computers to write poetry. What changed is scale: hyperscaling, specialized chips, and massive datasets.
  • AI models do not think or reason — they calculate probabilities to predict the most statistically plausible next output.
  • ML is a subset of AI that trains mathematical models on existing data to make predictions on new data.
  • Training data quality is the biggest determinant of model quality. 80% of ML project work is data preparation.
  • Inference is the output a trained model produces when given new input — it is a prediction, not a factual lookup.
  • Overfitting means memorising training data instead of learning generalisable patterns — always evaluate on held-out test data.
  • LLMs are FMs trained on trillions of words; they can generate text, answer questions, summarise, and produce code.
  • Hallucination is a fundamental property of LLMs — they produce plausible-sounding output that may be completely false. Always verify.
  • Responsible AI requires accuracy/safety, explainability/transparency, and governance/control — not as aspirations, but as engineering requirements.
  • Bias originates in training data; it perpetuates and amplifies existing societal inequalities when deployed at scale.
  • The AWS AI/ML stack: SageMaker (train from scratch) → Bedrock (managed model access) → PartyRock/Q (end-user apps).
  • Start with PartyRock (free, no-code) before touching Bedrock APIs — build intuition first, then code.

Project 1: Build Your First AI App in PartyRock

🎯 Objective: Build a working multi-widget AI application on PartyRock without writing any code.

⏱️ Time: 30–45 minutes

💰 Cost: Free — no AWS account required

  1. Navigate to partyrock.aws and sign in with Amazon, Google, or Apple account.

    📸 Screenshot: PartyRock login — choose your sign-in method.

  2. Click Build your own app and describe your app idea. For this project try: "A meal planner that takes a list of ingredients and suggests a recipe with estimated cooking time."

  3. PartyRock will auto-generate widgets. Review the generated layout — you should see an input widget (for ingredients) and an output widget (for the recipe).

    📸 Screenshot: Auto-generated PartyRock app layout with input and output widgets.

  4. Test the app by entering some ingredients (e.g., "chicken, lemon, garlic, rosemary") and observing the output. Try a few different inputs.

  5. Click the model selector on the output widget and switch to a different foundation model (e.g., from Claude to Llama). Run the same input again and compare outputs.

  6. Open the output widget settings and add a system prompt: "You are a nutritionist. Always include calorie estimates for each ingredient." Run the same input again and observe how the output changes.

  7. Click Share to generate a public link to your app. This is how you would share a prototype with a stakeholder without them needing any technical setup.

✅ Verification: You have successfully completed this project when you can demonstrate the same input producing different outputs across two different models, and a noticeably different output when the system prompt is changed.

Project 2: Amazon Q Developer — AI Coding Assistant

🎯 Objective: Install Amazon Q Developer and use it to explain, generate, and review code.

⏱️ Time: 20–30 minutes

💰 Cost: Free tier — requires AWS Builder ID (free, no credit card)

  1. Create a free AWS Builder ID at profile.aws.amazon.com if you do not have one.

  2. Open VS Code and go to the Extensions marketplace. Search for Amazon Q and install the official Amazon Q extension.

    📸 Screenshot: Installing Amazon Q in VS Code Extensions marketplace.

  3. Sign in with your Builder ID when prompted. You should see the Amazon Q icon appear in the left sidebar.

  4. Create a new Python file called test_classifier.py. Write this comment and wait for Q to suggest code:

    # Function that takes a list of numbers and returns the mean, median, and mode

  5. Accept Q's suggestion with Tab. Then highlight the generated function and right-click → Amazon Q → Explain. Read Q's explanation of what the code does.

  6. With the function still highlighted, right-click → Amazon Q → Review. Q will flag any issues including potential bugs, missing error handling, or security concerns.

    📸 Screenshot: Amazon Q code review showing a missing input validation suggestion.

  7. Open the Amazon Q chat panel and ask: "What is the difference between Amazon Bedrock and Amazon SageMaker? When would I use each?" Notice that Q gives AWS-specific, contextually relevant answers — it knows the AWS ecosystem.

✅ Verification: You have completed this project when you have used Amazon Q to generate code from a comment, explain existing code, and answer an AWS-specific question in the chat panel.
